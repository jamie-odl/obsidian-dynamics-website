BS 5228 and BS 7385, in plain English

Two British Standards govern most site monitoring: one for noise, one for vibration. This is the short version — what they ask for, and how a node turns them into limits you can actually watch.

This is a plain-language overview, not compliance advice. Always work to the consent conditions and the assessment of a qualified consultant for your site.

BS 5228 — noise (and vibration) on construction sites

BS 5228 is the code of practice for noise and vibration control on construction and open sites. In practice the part most people care about is the noise limit attached to a planning consent: a level, measured as LAeq (an average sound level in dB(A) over a period), that the works must not exceed.

The catch is that the limit usually changes through the day. Daytime working hours get one figure; evenings and nights get tighter ones. A single number is never the whole picture — the time of day matters as much as the level.

BS 7385 — vibration in buildings

BS 7385 deals with vibration and the risk of cosmetic or structural damage to buildings. The headline measurement is peak particle velocity (PPV) in mm/s, captured by a geophone. Guidance values depend on the type of structure and the frequency of the vibration — a modern frame tolerates more than a sensitive or historic structure.

Turning standards into live limits

On their own, the standards are documents. To use them you turn them into a limit profile: a noise figure per period (day / evening / night), a PPV value for the structures nearby, and the working hours that decide which period applies. The Mk.II Node stores that profile and checks every reading against it.

  • Per-period thresholds so a level that is fine at 11am is flagged at 11pm.
  • Working-hours rules so out-of-hours activity stands out rather than hiding in an average.
  • Escalation when a breach is approached or goes unacknowledged.
  • Signed evidence so the record you hand a client or authority can be verified.

The point is not to replace the consultant. It is to make the standard something a site team can see in real time, and something an auditor can trust after the fact.
