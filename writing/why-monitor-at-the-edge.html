Why we evaluate limits at the edge

On a live site, the network is the first thing to fail and the last thing to come back. So the Mk.II Node decides whether a limit has been breached on the device itself — before anything touches the cloud.

The cloud is not where the breach happens

A breach happens at the sensor: a piling rig swings, a slab drops, a generator runs through the night. If your only check lives in a server somewhere, every breach decision is hostage to a 4G signal behind a tower crane. That is exactly when connectivity is worst.

So the node carries the limits with it. Day, evening and night thresholds for noise and vibration are stored locally and evaluated as readings arrive. A breach is detected on-site, immediately, whether or not the link is up.

Buffer first, stream second

Readings are written to local storage and batched for upload. When the link drops, the node keeps measuring and keeps deciding; when it returns, the buffer is backfilled so the record has no holes. The cloud is the system of record and the place you read from — not a single point of failure for detection.

What the cloud is still for

Plenty. Storage and retention, dashboards, fleet maps, role-based access, scheduled reporting, escalation when a breach goes unacknowledged, and signed evidence exports. The split is simple: decide at the edge, remember and report in the cloud.

It is the same reason the node has its own power. A Li-ion UPS means a mains blip does not become a gap in your compliance record. Resilience is not a feature you bolt on later; it is the point of putting a computer on site at all.
